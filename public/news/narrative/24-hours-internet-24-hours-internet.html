24 hours off the internet & 24 hours on the internet

OFF THE INTERNET:

I danced nervously in a strange kitchen, throwing my arms in the air, moving my legs without purpose. I wore only boxers and a t-shirt as a dog named Maggie stared at me lazily, softly chewing a plastic bone.

I had made it four hours without the internet. The first three hours I spent biking slow from Olympia to Tenino.

I expected rain. I was lucky it started halfway to my destination.

It was my longest bike ride ever: about 15 miles. By the end I was dripping with sweat and rainwater, my back angry and my legs shaking.

I took off the outer layer of clothes and put them on a chair to dry.

I stood in the middle of the kitchen in a house in the middle of the woods. I wanted to read the news.

Early that morning I had decided that while I was house-sitting in a remote, secluded setting, I would isolate myself from my usual habits and interactions. I would avoid the interwebs. At least for 24 hours. But I still wanted to read news.

I would not bike to town to buy a newspaper, that would mean more biking.

I would not watch the news on the television, that would feel like cheating.

Also: I forgot about radio.

I would not read, see, or hear the news.

Every few minutes I would feel some part of my brain twitch, I would feel myself moving as if I were about to sit at a computer and check my email. But I was just sitting on a couch, reading a book. Feeling relaxed.

Or I'd be looking out a window, watching a pond do the things that ponds do. I sat on the porch, watching the pond, eating an avocado sandwhich with honey and chili paste, looking at trees.

I felt uncomfortable not knowing what was going on in Olympia. I wanted news about our country's new president, about the state legislative session, about whether or not the Reef would be rebuilt after the fire.

I felt empty without all that information. Once night came I felt comfortable being empty. That night I had a serious sleep.

I slept in the next day well past 10 a.m., making my time without the internet something like 25 hours.

Then I checked my emails, rss reader, and usual news websites.

It felt great.

 

ON THE INTERNET:

I had no choice but to cuddle with an extremely small laptop, balled up in a chair with a sleeping bag tent, using the computer's heat and my breath to stay warm.

From 4 to 6 a.m., I forgot how to find new things on the interwebs.

I got stuck checking my emails, waiting for RSS feeds to update, reading boingboing.net posts over and over.

I was a few hours away from spending 24 hours on the internet, and I felt beaten.

I started at noon on a Friday, thinking this: 24 hours without the internet was such fun, I needed to experience the opposite.

By 2:30 p.m. I felt sore. By 1:30 a.m. my body had an honest ache, and was rejecting my chair every second I sat staring at the computer screen. After 2 a.m. I got angry. I hated myself, everyone I dealt with on a daily basis, people I'd never met before, the internet. And I have no idea what I was doing that whole time.

There's a record of a few things. I started a blog about my 24 hours of interwebbing that holds a total of six inconsequential posts. I tried to use Twitter a few times to alert the world to my all-night project.

The only thing I truly remember was something about wizards.

I watched a video lecture about the programming language LISP, given by a professor at MIT.

What I remember most: He compared computer science and programming to magic and wizardry.

"Finally," I thought, "someone that understands."

It's a simple comparison: both practices utilize a set of commands, often conveyed through a specialized language, to complete tasks that range from making daily life simpler to creating wondrous, compelling spectacles.

I went to a search engine and entered phrases like "dark magic hacking," and "code spell wizard programming."

So when I curled beneath a blanket at 4 a.m., shivering from being awake all night in a cold house, I tricked myself into believing I was burrowing a cave, building a den of alchemy and incantations. I brought inanimate objects to life, commanded ghostly images to move, fight, flicker, vanish. I had the power of a wizard.

I was browsing Facebook profiles.
